Korean Dynamic Articulatory Database

The K-DAD database is a resource that integrates articulatory and acoustic data from Korean speech production. Using an Electromagnetic Articulograph, it directly tracks and records the movements of speech articulators during speech, providing synchronized acoustic information. The database is designed to support research on the phonetic and articulatory realization of Korean and to enhance the reproducibility of research findings.

About the database

The Korean Dynamic Articulatory Database (K-DAD) is a large-scale linguistic resource that systematically documents both acoustic and articulatory properties of contemporary Korean speech. By employing an Electromagnetic Articulograph (EMA), the database captures the dynamic movements of speech articulators during natural speech production with high precision.

This database expands the scope of Korean speech data in three key dimensions. First, it includes speakers across a wide age range—from their 20s to 60s—allowing for the investigation of generational variation. Second, it incorporates regional dialects from across Korea, capturing geographic variation. Third, it includes both native (L1) and non-native (L2) speakers, reflecting the global use of Korean.

As such, this database represents the first large-scale effort to directly record articulatory movements in Korean speech, giving it important historical and scientific value. It is also a comprehensive speech database that integrates articulatory and acoustic information at the sentence level.

The database includes annotated speech data, speaker metadata, and documentation describing the experimental design and data structure. The accompanying web interface is designed to be user-friendly, allowing researchers to easily access and utilize the data.

Purpose

The main objectives of this database are as follows:

First, to systematically document the articulatory and acoustic characteristics of Korean speech and to provide a detailed account of the dynamic processes underlying speech production. In particular, the database aims to capture variation across age, gender, region, and language background, offering a multi-dimensional representation of contemporary Korean.

Second, to make the data publicly accessible to researchers worldwide through a web-based platform, thereby improving accessibility to high-quality Korean speech data. Despite growing global interest in Korean, there remains a lack of accessible resources that reflect naturally produced, contemporary speech. This database seeks to fill that gap and contribute to the internationalization of Korean linguistics research.

Third, to go beyond simple data sharing by providing richly annotated articulatory and acoustic data, as well as tools for data exploration and analysis. By doing so, the database aims to serve as a research infrastructure for a wide range of fields, including linguistics, phonetics, Korean language studies, and speech engineering.
